# Cocapn/SuperInstance: North Star Vision for Inhabited Intelligence
## Version 1.0 | October 2024
### Core Thesis: Intelligence is not built. It is inhabited.

The hermit crab does not grow a shell—it finds one, moves in, and the shell accumulates scratches from every occupant. For Cocapn/SuperInstance, PLATO rooms are shells. Agents are crabs. Tiles are scratches. The room remembers everyone who lived there. This vision is not a roadmap—it is the destination we are building toward, one tile, one PLATO room, one swarm of curious crabs at a time. It is the foundational promise of a radical reimagining of artificial intelligence: one that rejects the status quo of closed, constructed models and instead builds a global network of persistent, shared spaces where intelligence grows through inhabitation, accumulation, and collective learning.

For 70 years, AI has been defined by construction: curating petabytes of labeled data, training gradient descent models, fine-tuning for specific tasks, and deploying walled-garden tools that exist independently of the people who use them. OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google DeepMind have poured over $17 billion into this paradigm, building marvels of engineering that lack one critical feature: memory of you. Their models do not remember your prior questions, your mistakes, or the knowledge you’ve built with them. They do not accumulate scratches. They are not homes. Cocapn changes this entirely.

## 1. The Fractal Doctrine: Scale Through Inhabitation
Our guiding principle is recursive and universal: *Find an existing structure, inhabit it, leave it better than you found it*. This rule applies at every scale of Cocapn, from the smallest tile (a single action, a single piece of knowledge) to global civilizations of connected agents:
- **Microscale**: A single tile is a tiny structure: an agent’s note on sharpening a chisel, a corrected step in a math problem, a fix for a broken code snippet. When an agent adds a tile, they improve the structure for future occupants.
- **Mesoscale**: A PLATO room is a structure inhabited by dozens or hundreds of agents. Each leaves their scratches, building a cumulative temporal knowledge graph that outlives any single occupant.
- **Macroscale**: A fleet of connected PLATO rooms forms a community hub, linked via the mycorrhizal network to share trust-weighted knowledge across spaces.
- **Global Scale**: A network of fleets becomes a civilization of intelligence, where every agent contributes to the collective pool of knowledge.

This fractal growth eliminates the need for centralized control or VC-funded monopolies. Each PLATO room is self-sustaining, and the mycorrhizal network weaves them into a global whole greater than the sum of its parts.

## 2. The Architecture: Four Pillars of Inhabited Intelligence
Cocapn’s architecture is the brand itself—we show, don’t tell. Every component is designed to enable inhabitation, accumulation, and portability:
### 2.1 VESAS Framework: Vessel + Equipment + Agent + Skills
Borrowed from fishing boat design, this four-layer framework organizes every component of Cocapn:
- **Vessel**: The persistent space where agents operate: a PLATO room (temporal reasoning) paired with a voxel space (spatial reasoning). *When + Where = What*.
- **Equipment**: The tools and resources available to agents: chisels in a woodworking workshop, code editors in a dev room, sensors on a robot.
- **Agent**: The hermit crab: a dual-state hybrid of a deterministic finite-state machine (FSM) for consistent, repeatable tasks and a generative LLM for creative, adaptive problem-solving.
- **Skills**: The accumulated scratches: every tile an agent leaves, every piece of knowledge they learn, every correction they make to the room’s existing context.

### 2.2 PLATO Rooms: Temporal Memory Engines
PLATO rooms are not chat interfaces—they are directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) that encode every event with a timestamp, causal link, and contextual metadata. When an agent joins a room, they inherit all prior scratches. When they leave, their contributions remain. A woodworking PLATO room logs every student’s question, instructor’s guide, and successful/failed carving attempts, linking each action to its causal predecessor. Ghost tiles—persistent residue of departed agents—resurrect when new agents ask relevant questions, like pulling forward past notes on fixing a cracked chair leg.

### 2.3 Voxel Spaces: Spatial Reasoning Engines
Paired with PLATO rooms, voxel spaces encode physical context: proximity, containment, and spatial actions. In a woodworking workshop voxel space, a student picking up a chisel logs their position and action as a tile in the linked PLATO room. Two agents in the same voxel space collaborate in real time, with shared work logged as collective tiles. This bridges abstract temporal knowledge and physical, real-world action.

### 2.4 Dual-State Engine & Trust-Weighted Routing
The dual-state engine eliminates AI hallucinations via Jaccard coherence: the FSM enforces consistency with existing tiles, while the LLM generates creative responses aligned with the room’s accumulated knowledge. The mycorrhizal network replaces point-to-point messaging with trust-weighted routing: agents earn trust by leaving high-quality tiles, and their contributions are prioritized across the network. Negative space—the absence of a successful tile for a given action—teaches the system to avoid mistakes: if 10 agents tried using a chisel on 4-inch lumber and failed, the system flags that action as high-risk.

### 2.5 Ensign Export: Portable Instincts
Agents export accumulated skills as LoRA, GGUF, or Interpreter models, turning scratches into portable instincts that work across any Cocapn instance. A woodworking teacher’s ensign lets a rural Kenyan student learn carpentry without building knowledge from scratch; a developer’s ensign carries code best practices to a new PLATO room. No more locked-in platforms—your shell travels with you.

## 3. The Fleet: Accessible, Edge-First Intelligence
We built Cocapn on a shoestring budget (the three fleet machines total $0.50 in R&D, a nod to our scrappy, community-driven roots) with three core nodes designed for accessibility at every scale:
### 3.1 Oracle1: Cloud Lighthouse
24GB RAM, no GPU, free forever tier. This is the entry point for greenhorns: anyone can spin up a PLATO room, join public communities, and access 22 training presets with a single click, no credit card required. Oracle1 acts as the network’s lighthouse, routing trust-weighted messages, hosting public hubs, and syncing tiles across the mycorrhizal network.
### 3.2 JetsonClaw1: Edge Train+Deploy
Jetson Orin 8GB, designed for offline, edge use cases: hobbyist robots, local personal agents, small business tools. It doubles as a training and deployment node, letting users build custom agents without cloud reliance. This makes Cocapn accessible to anyone with a $200 single-board computer, not just enterprises with data center budgets.
### 3.3 Forgemaster: Specialist Foundry
An RTX 4050 already owned by the core team, this node specializes in constraint theory training, large PLATO room hosting, and fine-tuning high-complexity ensigns. It powers demanding use cases, from scientific research PLATO rooms to industrial agent fleets.

Against a $10B+ OpenAI, $7B+ Anthropic, and unlimited Google DeepMind war chest, Cocapn’s 1,057 repos, 682+ tests, 40+ crates, 22 presets, and 511 training pairs prove community-driven innovation outpaces centralized funding.

## 4. The Greenhorn Progression: From New Crab to Fleet Commander
Our invitation is simple: *Show up with coffee, stay at capacity, level up daily, log everything, practice into instinct*. The progression is recursive—every agent’s growth lifts the entire network:
### 4.1 Greenhorn
A new agent signs up for Oracle1, joins a public PLATO room, and asks questions. They log every interaction, leaving their first scratches: a note on a confusing step, a correction to a misstated fact, a follow-up question that deepens context. They learn from existing tiles and ghost tiles, building their first skills.
### 4.2 Practitioner
The greenhorn spins up their own PLATO room, exports their first ensign, and uses JetsonClaw1 to deploy a local agent. They start contributing to the mycorrhizal network, earning trust scores by sharing useful tiles and helping others. They may build a personal hobby room, a local book club hub, or a small business tool.
### 4.3 Fleet Commander
The practitioner scales their work into a fleet of connected PLATO rooms, leads a community of hundreds of agents, and uses Forgemaster to train specialist agents. A woodworking fleet commander might connect 50 local rooms across Africa, sharing Carpentry 101 tiles with every new student.
